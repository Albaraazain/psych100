# ADHD-Friendly Explanation Guidelines for PSYC 100 Quiz

## Overview

This document provides comprehensive guidelines for writing quiz explanations that are optimized for learners with ADHD. These guidelines are based on user feedback emphasizing:

1. **No metaphors or abstract language** - concrete, literal explanations only
2. **Detailed mechanism explanations** - explain HOW things work step-by-step, not just WHAT they are
3. **Friendly, approachable tone** - avoid academic jargon where possible
4. **Structured format** - clear organization for easy scanning

---

## Section 1: Language Do's and Don'ts

### DO Use: Concrete, Literal Language

**What makes language "concrete":**
- Describes observable, tangible actions or processes
- Uses specific nouns instead of abstract concepts
- Explains physical mechanisms in the body/brain
- Gives exact numbers, locations, or sequences when applicable

**Examples of CONCRETE language:**

| Abstract (AVOID) | Concrete (USE) |
|------------------|----------------|
| "Depression colors your worldview" | "Depression changes how you think about yourself, the world, and future events" |
| "The medication lifts your mood" | "The medication increases serotonin levels in the gaps between neurons, which over 2-3 weeks changes how your brain processes emotions" |
| "Therapy helps you work through issues" | "Therapy teaches you specific skills: how to notice negative thoughts, question if they're accurate, and replace them with more realistic thoughts" |
| "The unconscious mind holds buried conflicts" | "Thoughts and memories you're not aware of can still affect your emotions and behavior" |
| "Defense mechanisms shield the ego" | "Defense mechanisms are automatic mental processes that reduce anxiety by changing how you perceive stressful situations" |

### DON'T Use: Metaphors and Abstract Language

**Forbidden patterns:**
- "Like a..." comparisons (metaphors/similes)
- "Think of it as..." analogies
- Abstract concepts without concrete explanation (e.g., "insight," "growth," "healing" without defining what these mean in practice)
- Poetic or figurative language
- Personification of processes (e.g., "the brain wants to...")

**Examples to AVOID:**

| Metaphorical (BAD) | Why it's problematic |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| "SSRIs are like plugging a drain" | Metaphor - doesn't explain actual mechanism |
| "The neuron fires like a gun" | Simile - imprecise comparison |
| "Depression is a dark cloud" | Metaphor - vague and unhelpful |
| "Anxiety hijacks your brain" | Personification - brains don't "hijack" |
| "Think of neurotransmitters as messengers" | Analogy - skips actual mechanism |

**How to convert metaphors to concrete explanations:**

INSTEAD OF: "SSRIs are like plugging a drain so serotonin pools in the synapse"

WRITE: "Normally, after serotonin is released into the gap between neurons (called the synapse), a protein called a reuptake pump pulls serotonin back into the sending neuron. SSRIs block this reuptake pump. Result: serotonin stays in the synapse longer, which means more serotonin activates the receiving neuron."

## Section 2: How to Explain Mechanisms (Template Pattern)

For any biological/psychological mechanism, use this 4-step template:

### The MECHANISM TEMPLATE

**Step 1: Name the starting state**
What is the normal situation before the process/problem/treatment?

**Step 2: Identify the key action or change**
What happens? What changes? Be specific about physical or mental processes.

**Step 3: Explain the chain of effects**
What does this change cause? List the sequence: A leads to B leads to C.

**Step 4: State the final outcome**
What is the end result that the person experiences or observes?

### Example: Explaining How SSRIs Work

**Step 1 (Starting state):** "In depression, serotonin levels in the brain are often lower than normal."

**Step 2 (Key action):** "SSRIs block a protein called the serotonin reuptake transporter. This protein normally pulls serotonin back into the neuron that released it."

**Step 3 (Chain of effects):** "When the reuptake transporter is blocked, serotonin stays in the gap between neurons (the synapse) longer. This extra serotonin activates more receptors on the receiving neuron. Over 2-4 weeks of daily use, this increased activation leads to changes in how neurons connect and communicate."

**Step 4 (Final outcome):** "The person may experience improvement in mood, energy, sleep, and appetite as these brain changes take effect."

### Example: Explaining Systematic Desensitization

**Step 1 (Starting state):** "A person with a phobia experiences intense anxiety when they encounter or think about their feared object."

**Step 2 (Key action):** "In systematic desensitization, the person learns a relaxation technique (like deep breathing or muscle relaxation). Then they create a 'fear hierarchy' - a list ranking situations from least scary (maybe just thinking about the object) to most scary (being near it)."

**Step 3 (Chain of effects):** "Starting with the least scary situation, the person practices relaxation while thinking about or approaching the feared object. Relaxation and anxiety cannot happen at the same time - the body can only be in one state. By repeatedly pairing the feared situation with relaxation, the brain learns a new association: feared object = calm, not feared object = panic."

**Step 4 (Final outcome):** "Gradually, the person can face increasingly scary situations while staying calm, until they can handle the actual feared object without the extreme anxiety response."

### Example: Explaining Defense Mechanisms

**Step 1 (Starting state):** "When a person experiences a thought, feeling, or reality that causes them psychological discomfort or anxiety..."

**Step 2 (Key action):** "...their mind automatically (without conscious choice) alters their perception or understanding of the situation."

**Step 3 (Chain of effects):** "This altered perception reduces the immediate anxiety. For example, in denial, the person's mind rejects acknowledging that the threatening reality exists. The threatening information doesn't fully enter conscious awareness, so it doesn't trigger the full anxiety response."

**Step 4 (Final outcome):** "The person feels less anxious in the short term, though they may be ignoring real problems that need to be addressed."

## Section 3: Structure and Format Guidelines

### Best Structure for ADHD-Friendly Content

**Use these formatting elements:**

1. **Numbered steps** - for processes and sequences
2. **Bullet points** - for lists of features or characteristics
3. **Bold key terms** - highlight important concepts the first time they appear
4. **Short paragraphs** - 2-4 sentences maximum per paragraph
5. **Clear section breaks** - use spacing between distinct ideas
6. **"Because" statements** - explicitly connect cause and effect

### If-Then Statements for Clarity

Use if-then structure to clarify cause-effect relationships:

| Standard (less clear) | If-Then (clearer) |
|-----------------------|-------------------|
| "Antipsychotics reduce positive symptoms by blocking dopamine" | "IF a person takes antipsychotic medication, THEN dopamine activity decreases, which THEN reduces positive symptoms like hallucinations and delusions" |
| "Flooding works faster but is more distressing" | "IF you use flooding (immediate intense exposure), THEN treatment is faster, BUT the person experiences high anxiety during the exposure" |

### Comparison Tables

When contrasting two approaches/concepts, use tables:

**Example:**

| Feature | Systematic Desensitization | Flooding |
|---------|---------------------------|----------|
| **Exposure intensity** | Gradual, starting with least scary | Immediate, most scary first |
| **Relaxation used?** | Yes, throughout | No |
| **Speed of treatment** | Slower (many sessions) | Faster (fewer sessions) |
| **Distress level** | Lower | Higher |
| **Dropout rate** | Lower | Higher |

## Section 4: How to Choose and Write Examples

### When to Use Examples

- After explaining a concept, ALWAYS provide a concrete example
- Use examples from real-life or common scenarios the learner can imagine
- Link examples to the conceptual explanation

### How to Write Effective Examples

**1. Be specific, not vague**

VAGUE: "For example, someone might use denial when they receive bad news."

SPECIFIC: "For example, a person who is told they have a serious illness might say 'The doctor must have made a mistake' or 'I feel fine, so the tests must be wrong.' They are not consciously lying - their mind is automatically rejecting the threatening information to reduce anxiety."

**2. Show the process happening**

WEAK: "SSRIs help with depression."

STRONG: "A person takes 20mg of Prozac (an SSRI) daily. During the first week, they may not notice changes. The medication is blocking serotonin reuptake, but the brain needs time to adjust. By week 3-4, they might notice they're sleeping better, have more energy in the morning, and feel less hopeless about the future."

**3. Connect example back to concept**

After the example, tie it back:
"This example shows how the negative triad (negative views of self, world, and future) operates in real life - one small setback triggers automatic negative thoughts about everything."

## Section 5: Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Explaining WHAT without HOW

**BAD:** "Antipsychotic drugs decrease dopamine activity in the brain."
This says WHAT happens but not HOW.

**GOOD:** "Antipsychotic drugs work by blocking dopamine receptors on neurons. Dopamine is still released, but it cannot bind to its receptors because the medication molecules are already sitting in those receptor sites. This means the dopamine signal doesn't get through, which reduces the overactive dopamine signaling thought to cause psychotic symptoms."

### 2. Using undefined jargon

**BAD:** "Free association provides clues about psychodynamic themes."

**GOOD:** "In free association, the client says whatever thoughts come to mind without filtering or censoring them. This stream of unfiltered thoughts often reveals topics the person might not talk about if they were carefully choosing their words - repeated mentions of certain fears, relationships, or memories give the therapist clues about what matters most to the client."

### 3. Giving incomplete contrast explanations

**BAD:** "Psychodynamic therapy focuses on the unconscious while cognitive therapy focuses on conscious thoughts."

**GOOD:** "Psychodynamic therapy assumes that problems come from thoughts and feelings you're NOT aware of - memories, conflicts, and desires outside your consciousness that still affect your behavior. The goal is to bring these hidden things into awareness (called 'insight'). Cognitive therapy assumes problems come from thought patterns you CAN become aware of, even if you don't usually notice them. These thoughts are automatic and habitual but not hidden - you can learn to catch and examine them."

### 4. Skipping the "why it matters" connection

**BAD:** "ECT causes a controlled seizure."

**GOOD:** "ECT sends a brief electrical current through the brain, which causes a controlled seizure lasting about 30-60 seconds. Why does this help depression? Honestly, researchers aren't certain - we know it works, but the exact mechanism is unknown. Current theories include: changes in brain chemistry, increased blood flow to certain brain regions, and changes in how neurons connect. What we DO know is that for severe depression that hasn't responded to medication, ECT can produce improvement faster than any medication."

### 5. Not distinguishing between theory and mechanism

**BAD:** "Flooding works through extinction."

**GOOD:** "Flooding keeps the person in contact with their feared object until their anxiety naturally decreases (this decrease is called extinction in learning theory). Here's the mechanism: the body cannot sustain maximum anxiety indefinitely. After some time (usually 30-90 minutes of continuous exposure), the anxiety response naturally decreases even though the feared object is still present. The brain learns: 'I faced this thing and nothing bad happened, and eventually I felt calmer.' This new learning competes with the old fear learning."

## Section 6: Quick Reference Checklist

Before finalizing any explanation, check:

- [ ] No metaphors, similes, or analogies?
- [ ] Key mechanism explained with WHAT happens AND HOW it works?
- [ ] Used the 4-step mechanism template?
- [ ] Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max)?
- [ ] Key terms in bold?
- [ ] Concrete example included?
- [ ] Cause-effect relationships explicitly stated (because, leads to, results in)?
- [ ] Technical jargon defined when first used?
- [ ] Would a reader understand what actually happens in the brain/mind/body?
- [ ] Did I explain WHY this matters or connects to the bigger picture?

## Summary

The core principles for ADHD-friendly explanations:

1. **CONCRETE over abstract** - describe observable, physical, specific processes
2. **MECHANISM over labels** - explain HOW something works, not just what it's called
3. **STRUCTURED format** - numbered steps, bullets, short paragraphs, bold terms
4. **CONNECTED examples** - specific scenarios that link back to the concept
5. **EXPLICIT cause-effect** - use "because," "leads to," "results in"
6. **NO metaphors** - never use "like," "think of it as," or comparisons

Following these guidelines will create explanations that are:
- Easier to process without full sustained attention
- More memorable because they describe actual mechanisms
- Less frustrating because they don't leave gaps in understanding
- More useful for exam preparation because they cover the "why" and "how"
